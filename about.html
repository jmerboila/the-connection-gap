About the project

A digital-literacy app for the children the network forgot.

DigiSkills PH is both a product and an argument: that connection is now a precondition for full citizenship, and that the tools meant to bridge the divide must be designed for the people on the far side of it — not the people who already have fast phones and faster internet.

The Founder

Jayson Mercado Erboila

MA, Digital Media & Global Communications
University of Niagara Falls Canada

I didn't read about the digital divide in a textbook — I grew up inside it.

In the Philippines, my family couldn't afford a computer for me and my siblings, so we did our school assignments at internet cafés, renting a machine by the hour. When things improved enough for us to finally own a computer, high-speed internet was still out of reach. And for a long stretch of my early working life, I couldn't even afford the smartphone my work expected me to have.

That distance — between what I needed and what I could afford — shaped everything that followed. I earned a BS in Information Technology at Mapúa (now Mapúa University) and built a career across web design, creative direction, UX design, and software testing, working with global companies in cybersecurity and financial services. Today I'm pursuing an MA in Digital Media and Global Communications at the University of Niagara Falls Canada. The through-line is simple: I've spent my career on the building side of technology and never forgot the side I started on — which is exactly why this site is built to be light, fast, and accessible, the way I wish everything had been back then.

DigiSkills PH is the project I wish had existed for that kid in the internet café. But the gap it addresses isn't only mine, and it isn't only the Philippines'. Every country has its own divide — rich and poor, old and young, connected and left behind. If you take one thing from this site, let it be this: look for the divide where you live. It's closer than you think, and it's never too small to start closing.

Objectives

What DigiSkills PH sets out to do.

Reach the unreachable

Deliver real learning to low-end devices and low-connectivity homes, offline-first, so a missing signal never means a missing lesson.

Teach safety first

Equip children with digital-citizenship skills — privacy, scams, kindness, misinformation — before they meet the open internet.

Close the skills gap

Move learners past mere access into genuine capability, targeting the second-level divide that access alone never fixes.

Scale responsibly

Grow in phases toward a full low-bandwidth learning platform, shipping only what a low-connectivity household can actually use.

Course concepts engaged

The digital-media ideas behind the build.

DigiSkills PH isn't only a project about digital media — it applies the field's core concepts in how it is designed, written, and structured.

Social impact

The digital divide

Treated as three nested levels — material access, usage skills, and tangible outcomes — and the social consequences for education, employment, and inclusion.

Digital citizenship

Online safety & literacy

Phase 1 is built directly on the elements of digital citizenship: privacy and data, security, cyberbullying awareness, respectful communication, and digital footprint.

Curation

Content curation & algorithms

Lessons are deliberately selected and sequenced. The project also teaches children how algorithmic curation — filter bubbles and bias — shapes what they see.

Emerging tech

Offline-first & mobile

On-device storage, background sync, and mobile-first design apply emerging-technology thinking to a context where the metaverse and 5G are still distant.

Future of media

Digital rights & access

Framed by the field's forward look — global reach and accessibility as the central opportunity, and a "digital bill of rights" treating connection as a right.

UX

Accessibility & design

This very site practises what it preaches: semantic structure, keyboard support, reduced-motion, and lightweight graphics that load fast on a weak connection.
